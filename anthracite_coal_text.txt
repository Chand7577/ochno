Section 2 — What is Calcined Anthracite Coal?
Calcined anthracite coal (CAC) is produced by heat-treating natural anthracite at high temperature — gas calcined (GCA) at ~1,280°C or electrically calcined (ECA) at up to 2,000°C. The process removes moisture and volatile matter, raising fixed carbon from the raw anthracite level of 85–92% to a consistent 90–95% in the finished product. Main production origins are Ningxia and Inner Mongolia, China.

Two types:
GCA (Gas Calcined Anthracite, ~1,280°C): F.C. 90–93%, S 0.3–0.5% — standard economy grade for general EAF and IF steelmaking
ECA (Electrically Calcined Anthracite, up to 2,000°C): F.C. 93–97%, S ≤0.3%, better conductivity — mid-range between GCA and GPC in both performance and cost

Section 4 — Where anthracite fits vs GPC
Anthracite is the right choice when:
You produce carbon steel, low-alloy steel or grey iron where nitrogen content is not a critical constraint
Cost per MT of carbon delivered matters more than absorption speed
You run a large EAF with a foaming slag practice — anthracite injected into EAF slag generates CO bubbles that foam the slag, stabilise the arc and reduce power and electrode consumption
You are blending with GPC to manage total recarburizer cost while maintaining acceptable quality

Anthracite is the wrong choice when:
You produce ductile iron — nitrogen above 0.3% in your recarburizer causes porosity every time
You produce low-nitrogen special steel (bearing, spring, tool steel)
You need maximum absorption speed per heat

Section 5 — Applications in India
EAF steelmaking (largest use): added during charging as foaming agent and carbon source; GCA standard for commodity long products (TMT bar, wire rod, angles); ECA for slightly better quality applications
Induction furnace steelmaking: general-purpose carbon steel billets; cost-sensitive IF operators in Mandi Gobindgarh, Raipur use CAC where sulphur and nitrogen specs are not tight
Grey iron foundries (cupola): added with coke charge for agricultural castings, manhole covers, counterweights — grey iron tolerates higher nitrogen than ductile iron
EAF slag foaming: CAC injected to maintain foaming slag — stabilises arc, reduces power consumption and electrode wear significantly
Carbon blending: many buyers blend 60–70% anthracite with 30–40% GPC — reduces cost while maintaining acceptable quality for most IF steel applications

Frequently Asked Questions
Q1. What is the difference between GCA and ECA anthracite?
GCA (gas calcined, ~1,280°C) reaches F.C. 90–93% — standard economy grade for general EAF and IF steelmaking. ECA (electrically calcined, up to 2,000°C) reaches F.C. 93–97% with better conductivity — a mid-range step up between GCA and GPC in both performance and price.

Q2. Can anthracite be used for ductile iron production?
No — anthracite nitrogen of 0.5–1.2% is far too high for ductile iron; nitrogen above 0.3% causes sub-surface porosity and poor nodule formation. For ductile iron, GPC with N ≤0.03% is the only safe carbon raiser.

Q3. Why is anthracite absorption rate lower than GPC?
Anthracite has a tight, non-graphitized carbon structure that dissolves more slowly in molten metal than GPC's graphite crystal. Factor this into your cost-per-MT-of-carbon-delivered calculation before comparing prices purely on a per-tonne basis.

Q4. What is EAF slag foaming and how does anthracite help?
Injecting anthracite into EAF slag generates CO gas that foams the slag — this covers the arc, reduces heat loss, lowers power consumption and cuts electrode wear. It is one of the most valuable uses of CAC and a genuine cost-saving practice in large EAF plants.
