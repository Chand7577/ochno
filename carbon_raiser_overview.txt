Section 2 — What is a carbon raiser?
A carbon raiser (also called recarburizer or carbon additive) is a high-carbon material added to molten metal during steelmaking or casting to precisely control and restore the carbon content of the final product.

During melting in an EAF, induction furnace or cupola, carbon is lost as the iron is refined. If final carbon is too low, the steel or casting will be weak, soft and brittle. A carbon raiser corrects this — accurately and efficiently.

Four parameters that define quality — at a glance:

Parameter
Why it matters
Fixed Carbon (F.C.) %
Higher = more actual carbon reaching the melt
Sulphur (S) % max

Excess N causes porosity and blowholes — critical for ductile iron

Frequently Asked Questions
Q1. What is a carbon raiser and why is it needed in steelmaking?
During melting, carbon is lost from molten metal. A carbon raiser restores it to the exact target percentage required for the steel or casting grade being produced.

Q2. Which carbon raiser has the best absorption rate?
GPC leads at 90–95%, followed by electrode scrap at 88–94% and anthracite at 75–85%. Higher absorption means less material per heat and lower actual cost per unit of carbon delivered.

Q3. Why does nitrogen matter in a carbon raiser?
In ductile iron, nitrogen above 80–100 ppm causes sub-surface porosity and poor nodule formation. GPC at 0.003–0.03% N is the only recarburizer that reliably keeps nitrogen low enough for quality ductile iron.

Q4. What particle size should I specify?
1–5mm is standard for EAF and induction furnace use — fast dissolution with minimal fines loss. 0–2mm suits ladle trim additions; 3–8mm suits large EAF or cupola operations.

Q5. What is the minimum order and lead time?
Minimum is 2mt for trials; standard container is 20–24 MT. Ex-stock grades deliver in 3–7 days after customs clearance; fresh import from China takes 30–40 days.
